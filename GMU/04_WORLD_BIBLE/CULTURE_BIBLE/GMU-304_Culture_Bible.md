# GMU-304 — Culture Bible

Customs, social norms, family structure, and rites of passage across the three powers. Scoped to what gives Season One's world texture without inventing detail no episode currently needs — expand per-kingdom sections as future chapters or characters require more specificity.

## 1. The Sultanate of Zamar

**Social values:** Learning, law, and reasoned argument are Zamar's highest social currency — a University-trained jurist commands more everyday respect than a wealthy merchant or a soldier, a reversal of Kasar Dutse's and Kwara's respective value systems. Public discourse and formal debate (echoed in the Diwan's deliberative process, GMU-309 §1) are culturally prized even outside political life.

**Family structure:** Extended-family households are the norm, with strong emphasis on the father/mother as household authority balanced against the wider Faith of the Covenant's communal obligations (GMU-305). Succession within ordinary families follows conventional inheritance custom; only the Sultanate's own throne carries the unique Diwan-ratification requirement (GMU-309 §1) — an ordinary Zamar family's inheritance is not gated by any equivalent council.

**Rites of passage:** A young person's completion of foundational religious and legal education (echoing real Islamic educational milestones — see GMU-305) is marked publicly, often at the University or a local sanctuary, and is treated as a genuine social threshold — the point at which a young adult's opinion begins to carry real weight in family and community matters.

**Everyday custom:** Zamar's hospitality culture is built around tea and formal greeting rituals (echoed in several characters' established "tea habit" — Yusuf, Bashir, and Amina all share this prop motif per GMU-005/GMU-507, a deliberate visual rhyme across kingdoms rather than a Zamar-exclusive custom). Public dress leans toward the indigo/gold-adjacent palette established in GMU-002, though ordinary citizens wear far less ornamented versions than court dress.

## 2. The Kingdom of Kasar Dutse

**Social values:** Honor, direct speech, and personal loyalty outrank formal credentials or written record — a war-captain's spoken word is treated as more binding than a signed document would be in Zamar. Kasar Dutse culture is deeply suspicious of intermediaries; a message or promise carried secondhand is worth less than one delivered face-to-face.

**Oral history:** Griots — professional oral historians and storytellers — hold major cultural standing, serving the function Zamar's written law and University scholarship serve: preserving precedent, genealogy, and the record of who has honored their word and who has not. A war-captain's reputation is, in practice, whatever the griots say it is.

**Family structure:** Loyalty networks often matter as much as bloodline — a family's standing is tied to which war-band it has historically fought alongside, and marriages between prominent families frequently function as alliance-building between war-bands, echoing (at a domestic scale) the same loyalty-based logic that governs the kingdom's succession (GMU-309 §2).

**Rites of passage:** A young Kasar Dutse fighter's first real combat, and survival of it, marks the transition to "Blooded" status (GMU-308 §2) — both a military rank and a full social coming-of-age, marked by public acknowledgment from the war-band's captain.

**Everyday custom:** Direct, unadorned speech is a cultural virtue — flattery or excessive formality (prized in Zamar's court culture) reads as evasive or untrustworthy in Kasar Dutse. Iron-grey and deep-red are common in everyday dress as well as military heraldry (GMU-308 §2), reflecting how thoroughly the kingdom's martial identity extends into civilian life.

## 3. The River Confederacy of Kwara

**Social values:** Reputation for fair dealing and reliability is Kwara's core social currency — since the Confederacy's entire strategy depends on being trusted by all sides equally (GMU-004 §2.3), a Kwara merchant's word on a contract carries enormous weight, and a reputation for sharp dealing (as opposed to hard-but-fair dealing) is genuinely damaging in ways it might not be elsewhere.

**Family structure:** Family trading houses are Kwara's basic social and economic unit — a person's surname often doubles as their house's brand (as with the Kwarafi name itself). Children are frequently apprenticed into the family trade young, and a house's standing on the Merchant-Council (GMU-309 §3) is treated as a multi-generational family project rather than one person's individual achievement.

**Rites of passage:** A young Kwara trader's first independently negotiated contract — however small — is treated as a genuine coming-of-age marker, celebrated within the house roughly the way a first campaign is celebrated in Kasar Dutse.

**Everyday custom:** Tea service as a recurring social ritual (shared with Zamar, per GMU-507's established prop motif) doubles in Kwara specifically as an informal negotiating custom — an offered tea service often signals a willingness to talk business, not just hospitality. Emerald and white dominate everyday dress in trading districts, with silver accents reserved for those of higher standing on the Merchant-Council.

## 4. Cross-Kingdom Note on the Faith of the Covenant

Per GMU-305 §6, all three kingdoms share the same faith (the Faith of the Covenant) but express it through different cultural lenses — Zamar's expression is scholarly and legalistic, Kasar Dutse's is martial and austere, and Kwara's is mercantile and pragmatic. This section should be read alongside GMU-305 rather than duplicated — religious custom belongs there; this document covers the surrounding secular social fabric each kingdom has built around that shared faith.
