# GMU-306 — Architecture Bible

Building types by kingdom and faith — palaces, gates, towers, markets, schools, bridges, villages. This document formalizes the architectural language GMU-002 §6 and the individual location briefs (GMU-501–510) already establish, giving it a single reference so any *new* building (not yet covered by an existing brief) can be designed consistently.

## 1. Zamar — Law-and-Learning Architecture

**Core language (per GMU-002 §6, GMU-007 §2.1):** Earth-toned walls (warm sand, ochre) with indigo and gold geometric tile inlay and carved lattice ornamentation. Deep-set lattice windows casting patterned light. Off-white stone base. Understated furnishings relative to the architecture — Zamar's buildings communicate authority through scale and craftsmanship, not raw ornament density.

**Palaces and official buildings:** Formal symmetry, layered courtyards moving from public to private (echoing the Diwan al-Ilm's own procedural, deliberative culture — see GMU-309 §1). The Palace's Throne Room (GMU-505) is the clearest expression of this: axial, ceremonial, designed to be approached rather than simply entered.

**Gates and towers:** City gates use the same lattice-and-tile language as the Palace but at civic scale — the Great Market's entrances (GMU-502) are deliberately less monumental than the Palace's, reflecting the market's neutral, non-royal status by centuries-old custom (GMU-004 §5).

**Schools (University of the Covenant):** Scholarly rather than ceremonial — more window, more open study courtyards, less gold inlay than the Palace, reflecting learning's practical rather than ostentatious character within Zamar's value system (echoing GMU-304 §1's social-values note that scholarship outranks wealth display).

**Markets:** Open-air, awninged, deliberately architecturally humble compared to the Palace or University — the Great Market's power comes from its neutrality and its crowds, not its buildings.

**Bridges:** Stone arch construction, minimal ornament, functional — Zamar builds infrastructure the way it practices law: solid, unglamorous, meant to last centuries without needing to impress anyone.

**Villages (e.g., Kantin Rafi, GMU-509):** Humble, sun-worn vernacular architecture per GMU-007 §2.5 — ochre and tan earth tones, weathered wood, no kingdom banners or official color-coding, deliberately distinct from the capital's polish.

## 2. Kasar Dutse — Fortified Mountain Architecture

**Core language (per GMU-002 §6, GMU-007 §2.2):** Deep red and iron-grey banners and accents against raw black and grey stone. Minimal ornamentation. Crossed-spear motifs. Defensive and martial in character, honor-code austerity rather than ceremonial richness.

**Palaces/keeps:** Tsauri Keep (GMU-503) sets the standard — built into the highland pass itself rather than beside it, prioritizing defensibility over grandeur. Interior spaces favor thick walls, narrow windows, and functional stone furnishing over Zamar's lattice-and-tile refinement.

**Gates and towers:** The most architecturally significant structures in Kasar Dutse, consistent with a kingdom whose entire economy and military doctrine (GMU-308 §2) centers on controlling a single mountain pass. Watchtowers along the pass are simple, load-bearing stone, built for sightlines and signal-fire visibility rather than display.

**Schools:** Kasar Dutse has no University-equivalent institution — per GMU-304 §2, oral history (griots) fills the role formal education plays in Zamar. What limited formal training exists (young fighters learning combat and mountain-craft) happens in war-band encampments, not dedicated school buildings.

**Markets:** Small, functional, taxed trade-post architecture along the pass — nothing like the Great Market's scale, since Kasar Dutse's economy runs on taxing passage rather than hosting commerce itself.

**Bridges:** Where Zamar's bridges are built to last centuries unnoticed, Kasar Dutse's mountain crossings are built to be defended or destroyed — rope-and-timber spans over the most treacherous sections, deliberately less permanent than stone, so a crossing can be cut in a real emergency.

**Villages:** Stone and timber, built into or against the mountainside rather than on open ground, reflecting a kingdom that has always built for defense first.

## 3. Kwara — Open Mercantile Architecture

**Core language (per GMU-002 §6, GMU-007 §2.3, GMU-507):** Light timber and pale stone construction, open canal-facing walls, emerald and white banners with silver accent trim. Transparency and liquidity of movement rather than monumentality.

**Merchant houses:** The Kwarafi Merchant House (GMU-507) sets the standard — airy, open, built for the visible movement of goods and information. Counting halls dominate over any equivalent to a throne room; Kwara's architecture literally has no seat of singular power to build around, consistent with its rotating Merchant-Council governance (GMU-309 §3).

**Gates and towers:** Minimal — Kwara has little use for fortification given it maintains no standing army (GMU-308 §3) and depends on being useful to everyone rather than defensible against anyone.

**Schools:** Apprenticeship-based rather than institutional (GMU-304 §3) — young traders learn inside family trading houses, so there is no Kwara equivalent to the University of the Covenant. A given house's counting hall functions as its own informal school.

**Markets:** Floating markets and canal-side stalls (GMU-507's Trade Quarter Canals) — Kwara's markets are literally built on and around water, distinct from Zamar's static Great Market or Kasar Dutse's fixed trade posts.

**Bridges:** Light timber footbridges and canal crossings, built for pedestrian and small-cargo movement rather than heavy load-bearing — consistent with a delta economy organized around boats rather than roads.

**Villages:** River-delta fishing and small-trade settlements, timber-built, clustered along canals rather than roads.

## 4. The Faith of the Covenant — Cross-Kingdom Sanctuary Architecture

Per GMU-305 (Religion Bible) and GMU-506 (Great Sanctuary brief), the faith's architecture is deliberately independent of all three kingdoms' color systems — neutral stone tones (sand, pale grey, warm white), restrained geometric carving, soaring pillars or high windows admitting clear unshadowed light. Grounded in real Sudano-Sahelian mosque architecture, including the sahn (courtyard) and mihrab (prayer-direction niche) established in GMU-506. Any new sanctuary built in Kasar Dutse or Kwara territory (per GMU-305 §6's decision that both kingdoms practice the same faith through their own cultural lens) should use this same neutral architectural language even when built from local materials — a Kasar Dutse sanctuary might use the kingdom's stone construction techniques, but should avoid deep red/iron-grey heraldry; a Kwara sanctuary might use timber construction, but should avoid emerald/white banner colors.

## 5. Cross-Kingdom Quick Reference

| Building Type | Zamar | Kasar Dutse | Kwara |
|---|---|---|---|
| Palace/seat of power | Layered courtyards, formal symmetry | Fortified keep built into terrain | Open counting hall, no singular throne |
| Defining material | Sand-tone stone, indigo/gold tile | Black/grey stone | Light timber, pale stone |
| Gates/towers | Civic lattice architecture | Primary architectural focus (pass defense) | Minimal — little fortification |
| Markets | Great Market — largest, neutral ground | Small taxed trade posts | Floating canal markets |
| Bridges | Permanent stone arches | Defensible rope/timber spans | Light pedestrian timber crossings |
