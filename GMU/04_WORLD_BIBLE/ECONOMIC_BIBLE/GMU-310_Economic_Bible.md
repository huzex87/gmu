# GMU-310 — Economic Bible

Trade, currency, exports/imports, guilds, and taxation across the three powers. Formalizes the economic logic already implied by GMU-004's power descriptions and dramatized in the screenplays (Amina's dual financing, Kasar Dutse's pass tolls, the Great Market's neutral status).

## 1. Currency

No single unified currency exists across the Crescent — consistent with three powers that have never unified politically. Each power mints or recognizes its own coinage, with the Great Market of Birnin Zamar (GMU-004 §5) functioning as the de facto exchange point where all three currencies are accepted and informally rated against each other by the merchants operating there.

- **Zamar:** Coinage struck under Diwan oversight (part of the Diwan's broader legal authority, GMU-309 §1) — trusted for its consistency, since debasement would undermine the same institutional legitimacy the Diwan's ratification power depends on.
- **Kasar Dutse:** Coinage minted more irregularly, tied to whichever war-captain or King currently controls the mint — accepted at a discount outside the kingdom's own borders, reflecting its less centralized institutional backing.
- **Kwara:** Rather than relying solely on its own coinage, the Merchant-Council's trading houses operate a network of promissory notes and letters of credit between city-states — Kwara's real financial innovation, and the mechanism by which Amina can "quietly finance both sides" (per her established Season One arc) without physically moving cartloads of coin across contested territory.

## 2. Trade Routes and Goods

**The mountain pass (Kasar Dutse):** The only viable route connecting northern desert trade to the southern river delta (GMU-004 §2.2). Kasar Dutse taxes everything that moves through it — this toll is the kingdom's primary source of non-martial revenue, and the reason Zamar and the delta states have never been able to simply route around Kasar Dutse's territory.

**The river delta (Kwara):** Kwara's canals and river port at Kwarafi handle the bulk of goods movement once past the mountain pass — grain, textiles, and manufactured goods flow through Kwara's trading houses toward both Zamar and onward export, with the Confederacy taking a cut at every stage without ever needing to own the goods themselves.

**The Great Market (Zamar):** The endpoint and clearing-house for both routes — technically neutral ground by centuries-old custom (GMU-004 §5), where merchants from all three powers trade side by side even during periods of political tension, since disrupting the market would hurt everyone's interests simultaneously.

**Exports (per GMU-004's Master Encyclopedia worked example, Birnin Zamar):** Zamar exports leather, books, textiles, gold, and horse equipment — consistent with a kingdom built on scholarship (books) and craft (leather, textiles) rather than raw resource extraction. Kasar Dutse's exports center on what the mountains themselves provide (ore, stone, horses bred for mountain terrain) plus toll revenue rather than manufactured goods. Kwara exports comparatively little of its own manufacture — its economy is built on moving and financing other kingdoms' goods rather than producing its own.

## 3. Guilds

Zamar is the only power with a formal guild structure, consistent with its law-and-institution-building culture (GMU-304 §1) — guilds for scribes, leatherworkers, and textile merchants operate under charters recognized (though not directly governed) by the Diwan, giving Zamar's economy the same institutional, precedent-based character as its legal system. Kasar Dutse has no equivalent — trade skills pass through family and war-band lines rather than chartered institutions, consistent with its preference for personal loyalty over written structure (GMU-309 §2). Kwara's closest equivalent is the trading house itself (GMU-304 §3) — not a guild in the Zamar sense, but a family-based economic unit that functions similarly for apprenticeship and standards purposes.

## 4. Taxation

- **Zamar:** Formal, codified taxation administered through the same legal apparatus that produces the Diwan's rulings — taxation and law are, in Zamar's self-image, the same instrument of orderly governance.
- **Kasar Dutse:** Taxation is almost entirely the pass toll (Section 2) plus informal tribute owed to the King by war-captains controlling productive territory — there is no equivalent to Zamar's codified tax law, consistent with the kingdom's unwritten-law political culture (GMU-309 §2).
- **Kwara:** No taxation in the conventional sense — the Merchant-Council's revenue comes from transaction fees and interest on the credit network (Section 1) rather than a levy on citizens, reinforcing Kwara's self-image as governing by price rather than by authority (GMU-004 §2.3).

## 5. Kwara's Mercenary Fleet — Funding Mechanics

Per GMU-308 §3, Kwara's naval/marine forces are hired, not raised — this section formalizes how that's paid for. Mercenary contracts are funded directly by whichever Merchant-Council representative holds the contract (typically to protect a specific trade route or enforce a specific commercial dispute), not from a general kingdom treasury. This is why Kwara's military capability scales up and down with trade volume and immediate need rather than existing as a fixed standing cost — consistent with the kingdom's entire structural philosophy of treating military capability as a line item rather than an institution (GMU-308 §3).

## 6. Cross-Kingdom Economic Relationships

Zamar and Kasar Dutse's relationship has been "two centuries of taxed trade — and tension" (GMU-004 §3) — Zamar depends on the pass for northern goods and pays Kasar Dutse's toll; Kasar Dutse depends on that toll revenue and has never had reason to cut off the trade that funds it, which is part of why open war between the two has never actually happened despite the tension. Kwara profits from both relationships simultaneously without needing either to resolve, consistent with its core strategy of "never being anyone's enemy and never quite being anyone's friend" (GMU-004 §2.3) — Season One's dramatization of Amina financing both Fatima and Malik (GMU-309 §3) is this same economic logic applied to a succession crisis rather than ordinary trade.
