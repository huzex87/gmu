# GMU-313 — GMU Calendar

Months, years, festivals, harvests, campaign seasons, and moon cycles. Built to be consistent with the **CU (Years of the Covenant)** dating already used throughout the World Bible timeline (GMU-004 §3) and the season's forty-day ratification clock (GMU-004 §4).

## 1. The Civil Calendar — Years of the Covenant (CU)

Per GMU-004 §3, the current era is dated in **Years of the Covenant**, beginning at year 1 CU with Sultan Adamu I's founding of Birnin Zamar and the first Diwan al-Ilm (211 CU in the existing timeline is therefore 211 years after that founding). This civil calendar is solar, tracking the agricultural and campaign seasons all three kingdoms' economies depend on, and is used for administrative, legal, and military dating across the Crescent regardless of kingdom — it is Zamar's calendar by origin but has become the shared civil standard the same way the Old Tongue (GMU-312) became a shared ceremonial layer despite originating before the current political division.

**Twelve months**, grouped into three broad seasons matching the region's real Sahel-inspired climate pattern (dry season, rains, harvest) rather than four temperate-zone seasons:

| Season | Months | Character |
|---|---|---|
| **The Dry** (4 months) | Zolmar, Rendu, Vaelith, Sharos | Low river levels, the mountain pass at its most easily crossed, the traditional campaign season for any military movement (GMU-308) |
| **The Rains** (4 months) | Morien, Ithuv, Kaelanu, Dunar | High water, canals at Kwarafi running full (Kwara's most active trading period), overland travel through Zamar's farmland difficult |
| **Harvest** (4 months) | Toranen, Anuwen, Shalith, Zolven | Agricultural harvest across Zamar's farmland, the Great Market's busiest trading period, and traditionally the safest season for the mountain pass before winter closes it |

## 2. The Religious Calendar

Per GMU-305 (Religion Bible), the Faith of the Covenant is explicitly Islam, and Islam's real religious calendar is lunar (the Hijri calendar), running independently of any solar civil calendar — this is a real, well-documented feature of Islamic timekeeping (lunar months drift roughly 11 days earlier each solar year) and should be preserved rather than flattened into the CU solar system. In-world, this means the Faith of the Covenant's clergy and devout observers track religious observance by a separate lunar count layered on top of the CU civil calendar, exactly as real Islamic societies maintain the Hijri calendar for religious purposes alongside a civil or agricultural calendar for administration. **Editorial guardrail (per GMU-004 §1's standing instruction):** do not invent specific fictional religious holy days or their fictional lunar names — where a screenplay needs a religious observance on-screen, treat it in general terms (a period of fasting, a communal prayer gathering, a pilgrimage custom) grounded in real, general Islamic practice rather than inventing specific doctrinal content.

## 3. Festivals and Civil Observances

- **Founding Day** (early Zolmar, start of the civil year) — commemorates Sultan Adamu I's founding of Birnin Zamar and the first Diwan al-Ilm (211 CU). Observed most formally in Zamar, acknowledged but not celebrated with equivalent weight in Kasar Dutse or Kwara.
- **The Pass Opening** (early Zolmar, Kasar Dutse) — marks the start of the Dry season's easier crossing conditions and, historically, the traditional start of the campaign season (GMU-308 §2) — a Kasar Dutse-specific observance with obvious military undertones.
- **The Flood Reckoning** (early Morien, Kwara) — marks the start of the Rains and the delta's shift into its most active trading period; a Kwara civic/commercial observance rather than a religious one, consistent with the Confederacy's generally secular-practical public culture (GMU-304 §3).
- **Harvest Court** (early Toranen, Zamar) — the Great Market's busiest ceremonial trading period, when the Sultan (or, during an interregnum, the Diwan) traditionally receives formal trade delegations from both other powers — a natural future setting for a Season Two political scene, since it's a built-in annual occasion for all three powers' representatives to be in the same place.

## 4. Campaign Seasons (per GMU-308)

Consistent with Kasar Dutse's mountain-warfare doctrine (GMU-308 §2), military campaigns are conventionally fought during the Dry season, when the pass is most easily crossed and cavalry movement across the highlands is least hindered — this is why King Garba's Season One pressure on the border, beginning as it does near the Sultan's death, reads as urgent rather than routine: he's acting outside the traditional campaign-season rhythm, which the Political Bible (GMU-309 §2) already establishes as a sign of just how significant an opening Adamu III's death represents to him.

## 5. The Forty-Day Ratification Clock (cross-reference)

Per GMU-004 §4, Zamar's forty-day ratification clock runs in ordinary civil days regardless of month or season boundaries — it is a fixed administrative deadline, not tied to any lunar or festival cycle, reinforcing its character as a purely legal-procedural mechanism (GMU-309 §1) rather than a ceremonial or religious one.
