# GMU-316 — GMU Clothing (Everyday/Civilian)

Everyday and civilian clothing by profession, kingdom, and season — complements GMU-307's rank/ceremonial coverage with the ordinary, unremarkable clothing background characters and working citizens actually wear day to day. Where GMU-307 answers "what does a noble or official wear," this document answers "what does everyone else wear."

## 1. Zamar — Everyday Dress

**Base garment logic:** Loose, breathable layered robes suited to savanna heat, modest coverage consistent with the Faith of the Covenant's general dress norms (GMU-305) — this should read as simply how people dress, not as a religious statement being made on-screen.

**By profession:**
- **Scribes/students:** Simpler, undyed or lightly dyed versions of scholarly robes, ink-stained sleeves as a naturalistic detail, echoing without replicating the formal Costume Bible markers for senior clergy/officials (GMU-307 §1).
- **Market vendors/craftspeople:** Practical, sleeve-rolled working versions of the same base robe, often marked by trade-specific wear (flour-dusted for grain sellers, dye-stained for textile workers).
- **Farmers:** Rougher, undyed homespun fabric, more heavily weathered than city dress, reflecting the plainer economic reality outside the capital (echoing GMU-315 §1's farmer-food parallel).

**Seasonal variation (per GMU-313's calendar):** Lighter, looser layering during the Dry season; slightly heavier, more water-resistant outer layers during the Rains, when overland travel through Zamar's farmland becomes difficult.

## 2. Kasar Dutse — Everyday Dress

**Base garment logic:** Layered, wind-resistant clothing suited to highland conditions — practical over decorative, consistent with the kingdom's honor-through-substance value system (GMU-304 §2). Iron-grey and deep-red remain common even outside formal or martial contexts (per GMU-307 §2), but everyday versions are unornamented, undyed-leather-and-wool practicality rather than heraldic display.

**By profession:**
- **Non-combatant highland workers (herders, stoneworkers):** Heavy wool and leather layering, minimal ornament, built for mountain labor and weather rather than any visual statement.
- **Griots (per GMU-304 §2, GMU-314 §2):** A modest but distinct exception to the kingdom's plain-dress norm — griots traditionally wear at least one visibly distinctive item (a particular wrap, sash, or ornament) marking their social role as history-keepers, since their standing is different in kind from a war-band fighter's.
- **Children/non-combatants generally:** Simplified, practical versions of adult dress — Kasar Dutse does not maintain a strong visual distinction between "everyday" and "training" dress for young people expected to eventually join a war-band.

**Seasonal variation:** Heavier wool layering through the highland's colder months (broadly overlapping with the Dry season per GMU-313, since highland elevation keeps temperatures cooler even when the lowlands are in dry-season heat); lighter layering during the Rains.

## 3. Kwara — Everyday Dress

**Base garment logic:** Light, practical clothing suited to a working port/canal economy — sleeves and hems cut to avoid catching on boat rigging or cargo, reflecting a population whose daily life happens on and around water (GMU-306 §3). Emerald and white remain present in everyday dress (per GMU-307 §3) but understated — a colored sash or trim rather than full garment saturation.

**By profession:**
- **Dockworkers/boat crews:** The most practical end of Kwara's dress spectrum — minimal ornament, fabric chosen for durability and quick-drying over appearance.
- **Clerks/trading-house apprentices (per GMU-304 §3):** Slightly more composed dress than dockworkers, reflecting their closer proximity to client-facing trade work, but still well short of a trading-house principal's tailored quality (GMU-307 §3).
- **Market/canal vendors:** Practical dress similar to dockworkers, often marked by small trade-specific details (a particular apron style for fish sellers, for instance).

**Seasonal variation:** Lighter dress during the Dry season's lower water levels; more water-resistant outer layers during the Rains, when the delta's canals run highest and Kwara's trading season is at its busiest (GMU-313 §1).

## 4. Cross-Kingdom Note on Modesty and Faith-Consistent Dress

Per GMU-305, all three kingdoms share the Faith of the Covenant, and everyday dress across all three reflects generally modest coverage consistent with that shared faith, expressed through each kingdom's own practical and cultural lens (per GMU-305 §6 and GMU-304 §4) — Zamar's version reads as scholarly-modest, Kasar Dutse's as martial-practical, Kwara's as work-practical. None of the three should be depicted as irreligious or immodest by contrast with the others; the differences are cultural expression, not differing levels of faith observance.
