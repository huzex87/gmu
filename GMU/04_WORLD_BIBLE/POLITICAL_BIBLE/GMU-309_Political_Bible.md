# GMU-309 — Political Bible

Formalizes the governance systems of all three powers — most of which the World Bible (GMU-004) and Season One's screenplays already establish through the story itself. This document exists to make those rules explicit and consistent for future writing, not to introduce new political mechanics the screenplays don't already rely on.

## 1. The Sultanate of Zamar — The Diwan al-Ilm and Ratification

**Composition.** The Diwan al-Ilm ("Council of Knowledge") is a body of senior **Ulama** — Islamic scholars and jurists (see GMU-305 §3 for the real-world grounding, Ahl al-Hall wal-Aqd) — drawn primarily from the University of the Covenant's most senior faculty and the Great Sanctuary's clergy hierarchy. Membership is not hereditary; it is earned through scholarly standing, meaning the Diwan renews itself generationally rather than passing seats down family lines. High Cleric Nafisa and Elders Kamal and Bashir (GMU-005) represent this body's senior tier in Season One.

**Core function — ratification.** Per GMU-004 §4, a new Sultan must be presented to and ratified by the Diwan within **forty days** of the previous Sultan's death, or the throne is considered vacant and open to challenge. Ratification is not a rubber stamp: the Diwan can and historically has withheld it, and the process involves the Diwan examining the claimant's fitness to rule (character, conduct, capacity) rather than simple bloodline priority — consistent with the real historical Ahl al-Hall wal-Aqd concept this system is grounded in (GMU-305).

**Procedure (as dramatized across Season One):** A claimant petitions the Diwan; the Diwan deliberates (privately, and — per Episode 6/Chapters 11-12 — sometimes meets separately with each claimant); a final vote is held before the forty-day clock expires (dramatized at the Episode 9/Chapters 17-18 ratification vote). The vote's exact mechanics (unanimous vs. majority, who may abstain) are deliberately left unspecified in canon so far — a future chapter or spec should define this only if a plot point requires knowing the precise threshold.

**Between successions:** When no Sultan is ratified, the Diwan itself functions as Zamar's acting authority — this is why the Diwan, not any single official, is the body Garba and Amina are both implicitly negotiating with/around during the interregnum.

## 2. The Kingdom of Kasar Dutse — War-Captain Succession

**The unwritten law (per GMU-004 §2.2, now formalized):** Kasar Dutse has no written succession statute. The throne passes to whichever claimant the sitting war-captains will collectively follow — in practice, a combination of combat reputation, existing alliances among war-bands, and a claimant's demonstrated willingness to lead campaigns personally rather than rule from the Keep.

**Why this system persists:** Kasar Dutse's self-image (GMU-004 §2.2 — honor, direct speech, oral history over written law) treats a written succession statute as a foreign, "Zamar-style" solution to a problem the kingdom considers better solved by direct demonstrated loyalty. A written law can be debated by scholars; a war-captain's loyalty cannot be argued away by a jurist, which Kasar Dutse considers a feature, not a flaw, of its own system.

**Practical mechanics:** A claimant to the Kasar Dutse throne effectively campaigns for war-captains' loyalty the way a claimant to Zamar's throne campaigns for Diwan ratification — through demonstrated fitness rather than a formal vote. King Garba Ironhand's own thirty-year rule (GMU-004 §2.2) reflects sustained war-captain confidence, not a one-time coronation event; this is also why Zamar's interregnum crisis is personally interesting to him beyond simple opportunism — it's the kind of instability his own throne could theoretically face if his war-captains' confidence in him ever broke.

**Court structure:** No Diwan-equivalent scholarly council. The King's inner circle is his most trusted war-captains, functioning as an informal war council rather than a formal governing body with defined membership or procedure.

## 3. The River Confederacy of Kwara — Merchant-Council and the Speaker

**Structure (per GMU-004 §2.3, now formalized):** Kwara is governed by a rotating **Merchant-Council** drawn from the leading trading houses of the river delta's city-states — membership tied to commercial standing (trade volume, financial reserves, guild leadership) rather than birthright or military record. Representation rotates on a schedule tied to each city-state's relative economic weight in a given cycle, keeping any single city-state from permanently dominating the Council.

**The Speaker.** In practice, the Confederacy is represented by whichever **Speaker** currently commands the most votes on the Merchant-Council (GMU-004 §2.3) — a role closer to "presiding officer with real influence" than "ruler." Amina Bello Kwarafi holds this position in Season One. The Speaker negotiates on the Confederacy's behalf, but major commitments (large loans, fleet contracts — see Military Bible §3) require Council approval, meaning Amina's power is real but never absolute in the way a Sultan's or King's formally is.

**Political philosophy in practice:** Kwara's governing principle — profit from everyone else's stability or chaos equally (GMU-004 §2.3) — means the Merchant-Council structurally discourages any Speaker from publicly committing Kwara to one claimant's side too early. Amina's Season One arc (financing both Zamar claimants while extracting better terms, per the character's established motivation) is the Speaker role functioning exactly as designed, not a personal scheme running against the system.

## 4. Diplomacy Between the Three Powers

No formal treaty body exists above the three powers — the Great Market of Birnin Zamar's neutral-ground custom (GMU-004 §5) is the closest thing to shared institutional ground, and it is customary, not codified. All Season One diplomacy therefore happens bilaterally: Zamar–Kasar Dutse tension over the border, Zamar–Kwara financial entanglement, and (more distantly) Kasar Dutse–Kwara's largely transactional non-relationship, since Kwara's rivers and Kasar Dutse's mountain passes rarely compete for the same trade.
