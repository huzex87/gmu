# GMU-314 — GMU Music

Per-kingdom instrumentation and repertoire — battle, wedding, coronation, market, funeral, and prayer music. This document expands GMU-007 §8's placeholder mood direction (already used for AI music generation) into a full reference with actual instrumentation, so composition — AI-generated or otherwise — has real material to work from instead of mood adjectives alone.

## 1. Zamar — Law-and-Learning Music

**Instrumentation:** String and wind instruments (per GMU-007 §8) — bowed and plucked strings evocative of West African and Islamic scholarly musical traditions, reed flutes, restrained percussion (hand drums used sparingly, for texture rather than drive). Zamar's music is written to be listened to and studied, not primarily danced to — consistent with the kingdom's scholarly self-image (GMU-304 §1).

**Court/ceremonial repertoire:** Formal processional pieces for Diwan sessions and state occasions — modal, dignified, deliberately restrained rather than triumphant (per GMU-007 §8's existing note), reflecting that Zamar's authority comes from deliberation, not conquest.

**Market music:** Livelier string-and-percussion pieces performed informally by street musicians at the Great Market — Zamar's one context where music is genuinely festive and commercial rather than scholarly, reflecting the market's unique neutral, cross-kingdom social space (GMU-004 §5).

**Wedding music:** Layered string ensembles with call-and-response vocal elements, celebratory but still measured relative to Kasar Dutse's more percussive wedding repertoire (Section 2).

**Funeral music:** Sparse, largely vocal, minimal instrumentation — reflecting the same restraint that governs the Faith of the Covenant's devotional music (Section 4).

**Faith of the Covenant devotional music:** See Section 4 — Zamar hosts the Great Sanctuary and is where devotional musical tradition is most visibly practiced, though the tradition itself is shared cross-kingdom.

## 2. Kasar Dutse — Martial Music

**Instrumentation:** Driving percussion-forward ensembles (per GMU-007 §8) — low brass or horn instruments carrying melodic lines over layered drums, with mountain-wind ambient texture used as a genuine sonic element (recorded/synthesized wind-through-stone texture) rather than metaphor.

**Battle/campaign music:** Percussion and horn calls used functionally as well as artistically — rhythmic patterns that double as real signaling (advance, hold, retreat), consistent with a culture that treats oral/aural transmission as more trustworthy than written record (GMU-304 §2's griot tradition).

**Wedding music:** More percussive and communal than Zamar's — group drumming and call-and-response chanting, reflecting marriages' function as alliance-building between war-bands (GMU-304 §2) rather than purely personal celebration.

**Funeral music:** Slow, low-brass-dominant laments, often performed by a griot rather than a formal ensemble — a fallen fighter's funeral music is, in effect, the oral-history tradition (GMU-304 §2) set to music, recounting the fighter's record rather than following a fixed liturgical form.

**Griot tradition (cross-reference GMU-304 §2):** Griots are Kasar Dutse's primary music-and-history keepers — a single griot with a stringed instrument and percussion accompaniment carries more cultural weight in this kingdom than a formal court ensemble would in Zamar.

## 3. Kwara — Mercantile Music

**Instrumentation:** Lighter, rhythmically fluid ensembles (per GMU-007 §8) — smaller string and light percussion combinations with genuine water-adjacent ambient sound design (recorded/synthesized flowing-water and canal-boat texture), charming rather than grand.

**Market/trade music:** Kwara's most characteristic music — informal, mobile ensembles performing in and around the floating markets (GMU-306 §3), designed to be pleasant background to negotiation rather than a focal performance, reinforcing the kingdom's "tea-service" style of hospitality-as-negotiation (GMU-304 §3).

**Wedding music:** Bright, rhythmically buoyant, incorporating the same water-adjacent textures as everyday market music — Kwara weddings, like Kwara commerce, favor charm and lightness over the gravity Zamar or Kasar Dutse bring to the same occasion.

**Funeral music:** More restrained than everyday Kwara music but still comparatively light relative to the other two kingdoms — reflecting a culture less oriented around ceremony and more around ongoing practical relationships (GMU-304 §3).

## 4. The Faith of the Covenant — Devotional Music (cross-kingdom)

Per GMU-007 §8 and GMU-305, devotional music is vocal-forward, using chant/call structures appropriate to real Islamic devotional music traditions (maqam melodic modes, nasheed-style unaccompanied or minimally-accompanied vocal forms) rather than invented "generic spiritual" music. **Editorial guardrail (per GMU-004 §1 and GMU-305's research-grounded standard):** any actual devotional music production should research real maqam and nasheed conventions rather than approximating them from imagination, the same standard already applied to GMU-305's doctrinal content. Since GMU-305 §6 establishes that Kasar Dutse and Kwara practice the same faith through their own cultural lens, devotional music in those kingdoms may incorporate local instrumentation (a Kasar Dutse devotional piece might use restrained percussion under the vocal line; a Kwara devotional piece might be performed dockside rather than in a formal sanctuary) while keeping the same vocal-forward, sparse-instrumentation core.
