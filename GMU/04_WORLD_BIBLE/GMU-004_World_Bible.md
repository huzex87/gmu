## 1. Setting Overview

The story is set on the continent of **Afrasah**, in the region known to its peoples as **the Sahel Crescent** â€” a band of savanna, river delta, and highland stretching between a northern desert and a southern coastline, inspired by the trade, scholarship, and statecraft of Sahelian civilization without depicting any single historical empire. Power in the Crescent has never rested with one throne. Instead it has moved, for over two centuries, between three centers, each built on a different source of legitimacy: law and learning, arms and land, or trade and gold.

**Religious basis (decision, 2026-07-10):** Consistent with the real history of the Sahel â€” home to genuinely Islamic states such as the Mali Empire, Songhai Empire, Kanem-Bornu, and the Sokoto Caliphate â€” the Sultanate of Zamar's dominant faith, referred to in-world as **the Faith of the Covenant**, is explicitly Islam. This is a deliberate worldbuilding choice, not an incidental one: Zamar's holy scripture is the **Qur'an**; its legal-moral framework is **Shari'ah**; its scholarly clergy class are the **Ulama**; and the Diwan al-Ilm's centuries-old practice of ratifying a new Sultan draws directly on the real historical Islamic governance concept of **Ahl al-Hall wal-Aqd** ("those who loose and bind") â€” the body of qualified scholars and notables historically responsible for selecting and confirming a ruler's legitimacy. "The Covenant" and "Diwan al-Ilm" function as this specific Sultanate's local names for its Islamic scholarly and legal institutions, in the same way real Islamic societies have developed distinct regional titles, schools, and councils while remaining part of the same faith. See `RELIGION_BIBLE/GMU-305_Religion_Bible.md` (drafted 2026-07-11, research-backed against real Islamic scholarship) for full doctrinal, devotional, and jurisprudential detail, including a new cross-kingdom decision on how Kasar Dutse and Kwara each practice the same faith through their own cultural lens.

**Editorial guidance for all future material:** Do not invent fictional Qur'anic verses, hadith, or specific fiqh (jurisprudential) rulings to justify plot points. Where the story needs a legal or religious mechanism (e.g., the forty-day ratification clock), root it in real, general Islamic governance concepts (shura/consultation, ijma/scholarly consensus, ahl al-hall wal-aqd) described in general terms, or treat it as Zamar's own historically-developed legal custom operating within an Islamic framework â€” the same way real Islamic states have developed distinct local legal traditions (e.g., different madhabs, or state-specific administrative law) without those customs being presented as universal Islamic doctrine. When in doubt, keep specifics fictional and general principles real.

## 2. The Three Powers

### 2.1 The Sultanate of Zamar

**Capital:** Birnin Zamar (see `MASTER_ENCYCLOPEDIA/ENTRY_00001_Birnin_Zamar.md`)
**Basis of power:** Law, scholarship, and religious legitimacy
**Symbol:** An open book beneath a compass rose, rendered in indigo and gold
**Colors:** Indigo, gold, white
**Government:** A Sultanate, but not an absolute one. Since the reign of Sultan Adamu I, no ruler has held the throne without ratification by the **Diwan al-Ilm** ("Council of Knowledge") â€” a body of senior Ulama (Islamic scholars and jurists) exercising a role modeled on the real historical practice of Ahl al-Hall wal-Aqd. This single rule is the hinge the entire Season One conflict turns on.
**Character:** Zamar sees itself as the moral center of the Crescent â€” the keeper of law, the seat of the University of the Covenant, home to the Great Market where every language of the region is spoken. It is also, quietly, the weakest of the three powers militarily, which is precisely why it has spent two centuries building a system where law substitutes for force.
**Current ruler (as Season One opens):** Sultan Adamu III has just died â€” suddenly, and without a Diwan-ratified heir named. His two children, Fatima and Malik, are now rival claimants (see GMU-005 Character Bible).

### 2.2 The Kingdom of Kasar Dutse

**Capital:** Tsauri Keep, built into the highland pass that gives the kingdom its name ("Land of Stone")
**Basis of power:** Military strength and control of the mountain trade passes
**Symbol:** Crossed spears over a stone peak, rendered in deep red and iron-grey
**Colors:** Deep red, iron-grey, black
**Government:** A martial monarchy. The King rules absolutely but only so long as he wins; Kasar Dutse's succession law is unwritten and brutally simple â€” the throne goes to whichever claimant the war-captains will follow.
**Character:** Kasar Dutse considers Zamar's rule-by-council decadent and slow, and considers itself the Crescent's true protector â€” it controls the only viable pass connecting the northern desert trade routes to the southern river delta, and taxes everything that moves through it. Its people prize honor, direct speech, and oral history carried by griots over written law.
**Current ruler:** King Garba Ironhand, mid-fifties, a shrewd and patient war-leader who has spent thirty years waiting for exactly the kind of opening Adamu III's death has created.

### 2.3 The River Confederacy of Kwara

**Seat:** The Port of Kwarafi, at the mouth of the delta
**Basis of power:** Trade, banking, and a mercenary fleet
**Symbol:** A silver river-fish on emerald and white
**Colors:** Emerald, white, silver
**Government:** Not a kingdom at all but a confederacy of merchant city-states along the river delta, governed by a rotating Merchant-Council and represented, in practice, by whichever Speaker currently commands the most votes.
**Character:** Kwara has no army worth the name and no interest in ruling anyone â€” it profits from everyone else's stability or chaos equally, and has built its entire strategy around never being anyone's enemy and never quite being anyone's friend. Where Zamar governs by law and Kasar Dutse by strength, Kwara governs by price.
**Current Speaker:** Amina Bello Kwarafi, in her forties, the most quietly dangerous person in the Crescent â€” she does not want the throne of Zamar or the passes of Kasar Dutse. She wants better terms, from whoever wins.

## 4. The Central Conflict

Zamar's law requires a new Sultan to be presented to and ratified by the Diwan al-Ilm within **forty days** of the previous Sultan's death, or the throne is considered vacant and open to challenge by any claimant with sufficient support â€” a deliberately vague clause that has never, in two centuries, been tested. Adamu III's death starts that forty-day clock with two rival claimants (his children Fatima and Malik), a foreign king watching the border for weakness (Garba), and a merchant power quietly financing both sides to see which investment pays off (Amina). See GMU-003 (Series Bible) for how this plays out across Season One.
