# GMU-315 — GMU Food

Per-kingdom cuisine — royal, street, farmer, festival, and soldier-ration food. Grounded in real Sahelian foodways (grains, legumes, dairy, and spice traditions of the region) without depicting any single real dish as belonging to a specific real culture, consistent with GMU-001's standing instruction to draw on real regional inspiration without claiming to represent any one historical people.

## 1. Zamar — Scholarly/Market Cuisine

**Governing principle:** Zamar's food culture centers on the Great Market (GMU-004 §5) as a genuine culinary crossroads — since merchants from all three powers trade there, Zamar's everyday food culture is the Crescent's most varied and cosmopolitan, reflecting the kingdom's self-image as where "every language of the region is spoken" (GMU-004 §2.1).

**Dietary basis (per GMU-305, Faith of the Covenant = Islam):** Halal dietary practice governs Zamar's food culture broadly — no pork, no alcohol, ritual slaughter for meat. This should be treated as an ordinary, unremarked-upon background fact of daily life (the way it functions in real Islamic societies) rather than a plot point to be explained on-screen.

**Royal/court cuisine:** Grain-based dishes (millet, sorghum) elevated with imported spices moving through the Great Market, slow-cooked meats, formal multi-course service reflecting the same deliberative, unhurried culture that governs Diwan proceedings (GMU-309 §1).

**Street food:** The Great Market's genuine culinary center of gravity — grilled skewered meats, fried grain fritters, spiced teas, sold by vendors from all three kingdoms side by side, the most literal expression of the market's neutral-ground status (GMU-004 §5).

**Farmer food:** Simple grain porridges, legumes, and vegetables from the farmland surrounding Birnin Zamar — the everyday diet most Zamar citizens outside the capital actually eat, plainer than either court or market food.

**Festival food:** Elaborate versions of court cuisine served communally during Harvest Court (GMU-313 §3) and other civil observances — richer spicing, shared serving dishes reflecting communal celebration.

## 2. Kasar Dutse — Martial/Highland Cuisine

**Governing principle:** Practical, calorie-dense, built for a population organized around campaign seasons (GMU-313 §4) and mountain physical labor rather than court refinement — Kasar Dutse's food culture prizes substance over presentation, consistent with its broader honor-through-substance value system (GMU-304 §2).

**Dietary basis:** Also governed by halal practice per the shared Faith of the Covenant (GMU-305 §6), though Kasar Dutse's version of the faith is martial and austere — religious dietary observance here reads as another expression of discipline rather than devotional refinement.

**Royal/war-captain cuisine:** Roasted meats (mountain-bred livestock), hearty grain breads, minimal elaborate spicing relative to Zamar — even King Garba's table should read as closer to a well-provisioned war camp than a Zamar-style royal court, consistent with his character design guardrail against ceremonial excess (GMU-007's existing note on Garba).

**Campaign rations:** Dense, portable, long-keeping foods — dried meat, hard grain bread, preserved dairy — built for war-bands moving through the pass, directly tied to the kingdom's mountain-warfare doctrine (GMU-308 §2).

**Everyday/farmer food:** Simple mountain-adapted grains and highland livestock dairy — plainer than court fare but not meaningfully different in kind, reflecting a kingdom without Zamar's sharp court/street culinary divide.

**Festival food:** Shared roasted-meat feasts tied to griot storytelling occasions (GMU-304 §2, GMU-314 §2) — food and oral history are consumed together at Kasar Dutse celebrations, reinforcing the culture's oral-transmission values.

## 3. Kwara — Delta/Trade Cuisine

**Governing principle:** River and delta-sourced food — fish, rice, and imported spice from Kwara's trade network — reflecting a kingdom whose entire identity is built on the movement of goods rather than local production (GMU-004 §2.3).

**Dietary basis:** Also halal per the shared faith (GMU-305 §6), practiced through Kwara's mercantile-pragmatic cultural lens — dietary observance here is treated as simply good, reliable business practice as much as devotion, consistent with the kingdom's overall pragmatic relationship to the faith.

**Royal/Speaker's-table cuisine:** Delta fish and rice dishes elevated with imported spice — Amina's table (GMU-507's tea-service prop motif, GMU-304 §3) should favor understated quality over lavish display, consistent with her established restraint.

**Street/dock food:** Grilled and fried fish, rice dishes, served dockside and canal-side — Kwara's most visible everyday food culture, eaten quickly amid ongoing trade rather than as a separate social occasion.

**Trading-house cuisine:** Tea service (GMU-304 §3's established negotiating-ritual prop) is the closest thing Kwara has to a signature culinary custom — food itself is secondary to the tea-as-business-ritual practice already established across Amina's, Yusuf's, and Bashir's characterization.

**Festival food:** Communal fish feasts tied to the Flood Reckoning (GMU-313 §3), marking the start of the delta's most active trading season.

## 4. Cross-Kingdom Note

All three kingdoms share halal dietary practice as a baseline (per the shared Faith of the Covenant, GMU-305 §6) — the meaningful differences between their food cultures are about preparation philosophy, formality, and what's locally available, not about which foods are permitted. This mirrors GMU-304 §4's cross-kingdom note on the faith: the underlying religious framework is shared, but each kingdom expresses it through a different cultural lens.
