# GIDAN MULKI UNIVERSE
## Season One, Chapter 02 — "Forty Days"

*Directly continues from Chapter 01 ("The Sultan's Silence") — same scene-day continuity, no time skip unless noted in the first scene heading below. Second half of the original Episode 1 ("The Sultan's Silence").*

### ACT ONE

**EXT. BIRNIN ZAMAR — GREAT MARKET — DAY**

The stalls are shuttered, awnings tied down — unheard of, on a trading day. Crowds line the processional road instead, silent, dressed in unbleached mourning cloth. Among them: DANLADI (24), Zamar cavalry lieutenant, off duty, standing with an OLD WOMAN who grips his arm like she needs the support more than the moment requires.

**OLD WOMAN**
Forty days, they're saying. To choose between the two of them like it's a market bid.

**DANLADI**
It's the law, auntie. It's kept us out of wars his grandfather's grandfather used to have every generation.

**OLD WOMAN**
(not unkindly, but pointed)
The law's never had to hold when both sides are people the soldiers actually love. Ask me how well that's ever gone anywhere else.

Danladi doesn't have an answer. In the distance, drums — slow, ceremonial — announce the funeral procession approaching.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. PALACE OF ZAMAR — PRIVATE ANTECHAMBER — DAY**

Fatima, now in unbleached mourning white over her indigo, sits across from ELDER BASHIR — seventies, gentle-eyed, the most senior member of the Diwan after Kamal, and by far the most trusted by the late Sultan. He pours tea for her himself, waving off a servant who tries to do it.

**BASHIR**
Your father used to say the Diwan feared you more than it feared Kasar Dutse's whole army. I told him that was an exaggeration. I'm no longer certain it was.

**FATIMA**
(a tired almost-smile)
Is that a compliment, Elder Bashir, or a warning?

**BASHIR**
At my age those are usually the same sentence.

(beat, more serious)

I want to tell you something, and I want you to understand I am not yet certain enough to say it in the Hall. There are ledgers from your father's final weeks I have not been permitted to see. I asked Yusuf for them twice. Twice, something more urgent came up.

Fatima goes very still.

**FATIMA**
You think someone's hiding something from the Diwan.

**BASHIR**
I think an old man is dead of an illness nobody can name, and the one man with access to every room he slept in has been unusually busy keeping me from his papers. That may be nothing. I have been wrong before, loudly, and lived to regret the volume.

(he takes her hand, briefly, paternal)

Be careful who you trust with what you don't yet know, Fatima. That is the only inheritance I have to offer you today.

**CUT TO:**

**EXT. ZAMAR CAVALRY GROUNDS — WAR TENT — DAY**

Malik, still in mourning white, stands before a map table with three senior GENERALS — older, harder men than the soldiers from Act One. GENERAL TAHIR, the most senior, speaks with the bluntness of a man who has earned it.

**GENERAL TAHIR**
Forty days is a courtesy your father's law affords the Diwan. It is not a courtesy Kasar Dutse's cavalry is going to extend us at the border pass. You have three thousand men who would follow you into the Diwan hall right now and settle this before sundown.

**MALIK**
And hand Garba Ironhand exactly the excuse he's praying for. The day Zamar settles its succession with soldiers instead of scholars is the day two hundred years of law stops meaning anything — to us or to him.

**GENERAL TAHIR**
Your sister will use every one of those two hundred years against you, word by word, until you're an old man standing in that hall with nothing left to say.

**MALIK**
(quiet, but final)
Then I lose that way. I won't win the other way.

Tahir studies him — not agreement, not yet, but something closer to reluctant respect. He nods once and says nothing further.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. PALACE OF ZAMAR — CORRIDOR — DAY**

Yusuf walks alone, unhurried, past servants who barely register him — his gift, and his danger, is being unremarkable. He stops at a servant's door, glances once down the corridor, and slips a small sealed letter into a departing MESSENGER's hand. No words exchanged. The messenger is gone before anyone could have noticed the exchange happened at all.

Yusuf continues on, straightens his sleeve, and enters the mourning hall as if he'd never paused.

**CUT TO:**

**EXT. TSAURI KEEP — KASAR DUTSE — DAY**

Cavalry assembling in the fortress yard — hundreds of riders, red banners snapping. GARBA rides the line once, unhurried, inspecting rather than rallying; he doesn't need to rally men who already know exactly why they're riding.

**GARBA**
(to a WAR-CAPTAIN riding beside him)
We hold at the pass. We do not cross the marker stones. Whatever happens in Birnin Zamar, Kasar Dutse did not start a war — we merely arrived early to whatever peace gets negotiated after.

**WAR-CAPTAIN**
And if Zamar reads it differently?

**GARBA**
Then Zamar will have to explain to its own Diwan why it went to war over stones we never crossed, while a scholar and a soldier were still deciding which of them gets to be blamed for it. I like our position considerably better than theirs.

The column begins to move out through the gate, banners first.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. PALACE OF ZAMAR — GREAT HALL — DUSK**

The formal mourning reception — nobles, clergy, foreign envoys in careful attendance. FATIMA and MALIK stand at opposite sides of their late father's empty ceremonial chair, receiving condolences separately, each performing composure for a room that is quietly, constantly measuring them against each other.

Their eyes meet once across the hall — the first time since the antechamber in Act One. Neither smiles. Neither looks away first, either.

**BASHIR (V.O.)**
(over the following, softly, from earlier)
Be careful who you trust with what you don't yet know.

Fatima's gaze shifts, briefly, to Yusuf, standing near the door, watching the room the way a man watches a room he has already decided how to describe to someone else later.

**SMASH CUT TO BLACK.**

**END OF ACT ONE.**

### ACT TWO

**INT. UNIVERSITY OF THE COVENANT — DIWAN LIBRARY — DAY**

Fatima moves along a shelf of ledgers with a YOUNG CLERGYMAN trailing her, clearly sent to observe rather than help.

**YOUNG CLERGYMAN**
Forgive me, Princess — but the Sword Oath. On ratification, the Sultan swears it standing, blade drawn, before the assembled Diwan and the army both. It has never once been sworn by —

**FATIMA**
(not looking up from the ledger)
By a woman. Say it. I've heard worse phrased more politely.

**YOUNG CLERGYMAN**
(flustered)
I only mean — the ceremony assumes —

**FATIMA**
The ceremony assumes a great many things that were true two hundred years ago and haven't been questioned since, because no one questioning them has ever stood close enough to the throne to make it costly to ignore them. I intend to be the first. If the Diwan's objection to me is a sentence in a ceremony, write me a new sentence. If the objection is something else wearing that sentence as a disguise, at least have the honesty to say so to my face.

The clergyman has no answer. Fatima finally looks up — not unkind, just unwilling to pretend the conversation was about the ceremony at all.

**CUT TO:**

**EXT. PALACE OF ZAMAR — MAIN GATE — DAY**

A river-barge procession arrives at the palace steps — emerald and white banners, understated but unmistakably expensive. SPEAKER AMINA BELLO KWARAFI (40s) steps down from a palanquin without waiting for a hand offered, already scanning the gathered court with the flat, pricing look of someone who has walked into a thousand rooms and never once needed to be impressed by one.

**STEWARD**
Speaker Kwarafi. The Confederacy honors us.

**AMINA**
(pleasant, unreadable)
The Confederacy sends its condolences. I've come in person because condolences delivered by letter tend to arrive after the terms that matter have already been decided without us. I'd rather not read about my own kingdom's interests secondhand.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. PALACE OF ZAMAR — RECEPTION ROOM — DAY**

Amina and Fatima, alone, tea between them that neither has touched. Amina has the ease of someone entirely at home in a room built to intimidate her.

**AMINA**
I'll save us both the courtly half hour. Kwara doesn't care who sits the throne of Zamar. We care what the river tariffs look like once someone does. Back your claim with Confederacy gold, quietly, and I promise you favorable terms the day you're ratified — before Kasar Dutse can offer Garba's version of the same thing to your brother.

**FATIMA**
(evenly)
And if I refuse gold with strings on it?

**AMINA**
Then I extend the same courtesy to your brother, and let the two of you fight it out the expensive way while Kwara profits from the shipping either side needs to move soldiers. I'd genuinely rather not, Princess. You strike me as someone who reads a contract before objecting to it, which is rarer among royalty than you'd think. But I didn't build the Confederacy's fortune on sentiment, and I'm not going to start now, on your father's mourning week.

Fatima studies her — not offended, weighing it, exactly as Amina clearly expected her to.

**FATIMA**
Send the terms in writing. I make no promises reading them.

**AMINA**
(the faintest real smile)
That's the first sensible answer anyone in this palace has given me all day.

She rises to leave, pauses at the door.

**AMINA (CONT'D)**
For what it's worth — I liked your father. He was the only Sultan in two hundred years who understood that Kwara doesn't need to be trusted to be useful. I'd be glad to say the same of his daughter, one day.

She goes. Fatima sits alone with the untouched tea for a long moment, doing arithmetic Amina would recognize instantly.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. PALACE OF ZAMAR — ELDER BASHIR'S STUDY — NIGHT**

Candlelight. BASHIR, alone, has finally gotten his hands on a stack of ledgers — Adamu III's final weeks, obtained by means the scene doesn't explain. He reads with growing stillness, then stops entirely on one page, rereading it twice.

He sets the ledger down, hand not quite steady, and reaches for a fresh sheet of paper to begin a letter.

**BASHIR**
(murmuring to himself)
Oh, old friend. What did you find.

He dips his pen. Behind him, unseen, the study door — which he is certain he bolted — is no longer bolted.

**SMASH CUT TO BLACK.**

**END OF ACT TWO.**

---

### ACT THREE

**INT. PALACE OF ZAMAR — ELDER BASHIR'S STUDY — DAWN**

The candle has burned down to nothing. A SERVANT enters with breakfast, the same small domestic ritual as the Teaser, and the audience already knows, sickly, what's coming before she does.

Bashir is slumped over his desk. The ledger is gone. The unfinished letter is gone. Only an overturned inkwell remains, black across the desk like a shadow that spread too far to be an accident.

The servant's tray hits the floor. This time, no one screams — the shot holds on her silence instead, which is worse.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. PALACE OF ZAMAR — CORRIDOR OUTSIDE THE STUDY — MORNING**

Fatima arrives at a run, past guards already gathering. She stops in the doorway. Sees him. Doesn't move for a long moment — this is Bashir's tea, poured by his own hand, his own gentle warning, all of it collapsing into this one small room.

Malik arrives seconds later, out of breath, and stops beside her. For once neither of them says anything about the throne at all.

**MALIK**
(quiet)
Was it his heart? He was old, Fatima. Old men die of nothing, sometimes.

**FATIMA**
(not looking away from the desk)
He came to me two days ago and told me someone had kept him from reading father's final ledgers. Twice.

(finally turning to Malik)

Those ledgers aren't on this desk, Malik. Neither is whatever he was writing when it happened.

Kamal appears in the doorway behind them, taking in the scene, his authority momentarily gone entirely out of him — this is a man who has just lost the closest thing he had to a peer.

**KAMAL**
(hoarse)
Nothing leaves this room until I say so. Nothing.

His eyes land on Fatima, then Malik, with something new in them — not suspicion of either of them specifically, but the dawning understanding that whatever the succession crisis was an hour ago, it is now also, unmistakably, a murder.

**FATIMA**
(low, to Malik alone)
Forty days was never the real clock, was it.

Malik has no answer. Behind them, unnoticed by anyone in the room, YUSUF arrives at the doorway last of all — pauses one beat longer than grief alone would require — and steps inside.

**SLOW PUSH IN ON YUSUF'S FACE — UNREADABLE.**

**FADE OUT.**

**END OF CHAPTER 02.**

## Notes on the Complete Episode (carried from the original Episode 1 draft)

- Episode 1 as drafted has absorbed the beat-sheet material originally scoped for Episode 2 in GMU-003 (Fatima's Diwan campaign, Amina's introduction and offer, Bashir's murder) — the story simply ran long once actually dramatized. GMU-003 Section 7 has been annotated to reflect this consolidation; Episode 2 now picks up with the murder investigation (previously slated to open Episode 3, "Blood and Ink"). This is a normal outline-to-script divergence, not a canon break — see GMU-003 for the reconciled version.
- Bashir's death lands specifically because Act Two spent real time making him warm and specific before Act Four kills him — resist compressing that setup in any future rewrite.
- Yusuf's Act Four entrance (arriving last, one beat too controlled) is the second and final behavioral seed this episode plants toward the Episode 9 reveal. Two is enough — a third would tip the hand too early.
- Amina's Act Three scene deliberately does NOT reveal she's already made or will make the same offer to Malik — that discovery is reserved for Fatima's confrontation in Kwarafi (Episode 5, "The Merchant's Price," per GMU-003).
- Scene economy for the full episode: 12 scenes across 4 locations (Birnin Zamar interiors/exteriors, Tsauri Keep, and the University) plus the Teaser — a reasonable, produceable episode length (est. 32–38 script pages at standard formatting density).
- Recommended next step per GMU-000's "just enough, just in time" principle: either GMU-002 (Creative & Brand Bible, now unblocked) or beginning Episode 2 proper. Both are viable; Episode 2 keeps momentum on proving the story works, GMU-002 starts converting the now-substantial narrative into visual identity.
