### TEASER

**FADE IN:**

**EXT. BIRNIN ZAMAR — RIVER PORT — PRE-DAWN**

Mist off the river. Fishing boats already out, lanterns swinging at their bows. The city rises behind them in silhouette — domes, the terraced white walls of the University, the gold-tipped minarets of the Great Market, all of it still dark except for one window, high in the Palace, burning with lamplight it shouldn't have at this hour.

A CALL TO PRAYER begins somewhere in the city. Ordinary. Unbroken. Nobody in the port looks up at the lit window. Nobody knows yet.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. PALACE OF ZAMAR — SULTAN'S CHAMBER — CONTINUOUS**

Close on a hand — SULTAN ADAMU III's, older than we'd expect, a signet ring loose on the finger — resting on an open ledger. Unfinished handwriting trails off mid-sentence.

A SERVANT GIRL, sixteen, enters with a morning tray. Stops.

The tray hits the floor before she screams.

**CUT TO BLACK.**

**MAIN TITLES.**

### ACT ONE

**INT. UNIVERSITY OF THE COVENANT — SCHOLARS' HALL — MORNING**

Sunlight through high lattice windows. FATIMA BINT ADAMU (29), indigo scholar's robe, gold jurist's sash, stands at a lectern mid-argument with three older DIWAN SCHOLARS, all men, all visibly unconvinced by her — though she is winning the argument on its merits and everyone in the room knows it.

**FATIMA**
(calm, precise)
The river tariff dispute isn't a question of Kwara's honesty. Kwara doesn't deal in honesty. It deals in terms. Ask them to swear an oath and you'll get a very sincere oath and a contract with four exits in it. Ask them for numbers instead, and you'll get the truth by accident.

**ELDER SCHOLAR (KAMAL)**
(dry)
You'd have us negotiate with merchants the way we negotiate with children.

**FATIMA**
I'd have us negotiate with merchants the way merchants negotiate with us. It's only insulting if you assume we were owed better.

A ripple of reluctant amusement. Kamal almost smiles — catches himself.

The doors at the far end of the hall BANG open. YUSUF AL-KANEM enters at a pace that is, for a man his age and station, close to a run. The room goes silent before he says a word. That alone tells Fatima everything.

**YUSUF**
(quiet, to Fatima alone)
Princess. Your father.

Fatima doesn't move for one full second. Then she does — past Yusuf, past the scholars, out the door, robe forgotten mid-stride on a bench behind her.

**CUT TO:**

**EXT. ZAMAR CAVALRY GROUNDS — MORNING**

Dust and noise. MALIK IBN ADAMU (26), in training leathers, disarms a sparring partner with a move that gets a genuine laugh out of the watching soldiers — this is a man they'd follow, and they know it, and he knows they know it.

**MALIK**
(grinning, offering a hand up)
You're leading with your shoulder. Everyone can see it coming but you.

**SOLDIER**
(taking the hand)
Everyone can see the prince's father complaining about his taxes too, doesn't stop him doing it.

Laughter. It dies fast — a RIDER comes through the gate at a gallop, nearly runs down two grooms getting to Malik.

**RIDER**
(breathless)
My prince. The Palace. It's the Sultan.

Malik's face doesn't fall so much as go completely still — the stillness of a man who has spent his whole life being told what he'll one day inherit, and has just realized "one day" was never going to announce itself politely.

**MALIK**
(already moving)
Get my horse.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. PALACE OF ZAMAR — SULTAN'S CHAMBER — DAY**

The body has been moved, the room set right, but nothing else has changed — the ledger is still open on the table. FATIMA stands over it, alone for a moment, reading her father's unfinished sentence like it might still resolve itself if she looks long enough.

MALIK enters behind her, still in training leathers, dust on his face. They look at each other across the room. For a moment they are not rivals. They are just two people who have lost their father in the same hour and have not yet had time to be told what that means for the Sultanate.

**MALIK**
(quiet)
Was it — did anyone say —

**FATIMA**
He went to sleep writing. That's all anyone's said.

**MALIK**
That's not an answer.

**FATIMA**
(finally looking at him)
No. It isn't.

He crosses to her. For a moment it looks like an embrace. Instead he picks up the ledger, closes it gently, the way you'd close a door on a room you're not ready to walk back into. Sets it down.

**MALIK**
(a small, exhausted laugh)
Forty days.

**FATIMA**
You know the law that well?

**MALIK**
(a beat — this lands harder than he expected)
I know the law that well because I always assumed it would be a formality. Father would name me, the Diwan would nod, we'd have a feast. I never once thought about the forty days like they were a countdown.

**FATIMA**
(carefully)
They're a countdown for both of us, Malik.

He looks at her — really looks, maybe for the first time considering that his sister might actually want this, not as a consolation, not as a scholar's abstract point of law, but the way he wants it.

**MALIK**
(not unkindly)
Is that a warning?

**FATIMA**
It's the truth. I'd rather you have it from me than hear it in the Diwan hall in front of Kamal's whole council.

A long beat. Something between them has just shifted — not broken, not yet, but shifted — and they both feel it.

**CUT TO:**

**INT. DIWAN HALL — LATER, DAY**

A formal chamber, tiered seating, the DIWAN AL-ILM — a dozen senior scholars, jurists, and Covenant clergy — assembled. KAMAL presides. Fatima and Malik stand at opposite ends of the floor, not by design, but the room has arranged itself that way anyway, and no one moves to fix it.

**KAMAL**
By the law of Sultan Adamu I, upheld unbroken for two hundred and sixteen years: the throne of Zamar is vacant upon the death of a Sultan until a successor is presented to and ratified by this Diwan. That presentation and ratification must occur within forty days, or the throne stands open to any claim the Diwan judges worthy of hearing.

(beat, looking between the siblings)

The clock begins today.

Fatima and Malik exchange one look across the hall — not hostile, not yet, but both of them doing the same silent arithmetic: forty days, one throne, and the person on the other end of this room is the only other person alive who understands exactly what that costs.

**CUT TO:**

**EXT. TSAURI KEEP — KASAR DUTSE — DAY**

Grey stone, red banners, wind. KING GARBA IRONHAND stands on a fortress wall overlooking the mountain pass that has made his kingdom rich for two hundred years. A MESSENGER kneels behind him, having just delivered the news.

Garba says nothing for a long moment. Then, almost to himself:

**GARBA**
Forty days.

**MESSENGER**
Yes, my king.

**GARBA**
(turning, already calling out)
Send word to the war-captains. All of them. We ride for the border pass within the week — for stability, we'll say. Zamar's house is on fire. I intend to be standing close enough to feel the warmth.

He looks out over the pass one more time — not with malice, but with the patient satisfaction of a man who has been waiting thirty years for a door to open, and has just watched it swing wide.

**GARBA (CONT'D)**
(quiet, to no one)
Let's see what a kingdom of scholars does with a countdown.

**SMASH CUT TO BLACK.**

**END OF ACT ONE.**
