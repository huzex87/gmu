# Location Concept Brief — Palace of Zamar

The Palace is Episode 1's most-used location, covering seven distinct interior sets plus the main gate exterior. This brief establishes the shared architectural language first, then specifies each room — keep every room visually consistent with this shared language so the Palace reads as one building across twelve scenes shot (in production terms) as entirely separate sets.

## Shared Architectural Language

Earth-toned walls with geometric ornamentation (carved lattice screens, tile inlay in indigo/gold geometric patterns), high ceilings, deep-set lattice windows that cast patterned light. Indigo and gold as the dominant palette throughout, with white stone or plaster as the neutral base. Furniture is low, dense-wood, minimally carved — understated relative to the architecture itself, consistent with GMU-001's note that Zamar substitutes law for military force and doesn't need to perform wealth as aggressively as Kasar Dutse performs strength.

## Room 1 — Sultan's Chamber (Teaser, Act One)

**Key elements:** A writing desk with an open ledger, unfinished handwriting visible. Personal, lived-in despite being royal — books, a worn chair, evidence of a scholar-Sultan rather than a warrior one. Morning light through lattice windows in the Teaser; unchanged but colder-feeling in the Act One revisit once the body has been moved.

> **AI image prompt:** Intimate royal study chamber, earth-toned walls with indigo and gold geometric tile inlay, carved lattice windows casting patterned morning light, a wooden writing desk with an open ledger and unfinished handwriting, worn scholarly furnishings, quiet and personal rather than grand, photorealistic interior, Sahel-inspired palace architecture.

## Room 2 — Diwan Hall (Act One)

**Key elements:** Formal tiered seating for the Diwan al-Ilm council, more overtly ceremonial than the Sultan's Chamber — this is the room where Zamar's law performs itself. Central floor space where petitioners/claimants stand. Should feel like a cross between a court of law and a religious council chamber.

> **AI image prompt:** Formal council chamber with tiered stone seating for a dozen robed scholars and clergy, open central floor, earth-toned walls with indigo and gold geometric ornamentation, tall lattice windows, solemn formal lighting, Sahel-inspired judicial-religious architecture, photorealistic wide interior shot.

## Room 3 — Great Hall (Act Two)

**Key elements:** The largest, most ceremonial room in the Palace — formal mourning reception scale, room for nobles, clergy, and foreign envoys. A late Sultan's empty ceremonial chair should be a visible focal point. Dusk lighting per the script.

> **AI image prompt:** Grand ceremonial palace hall, high ceilings, indigo and gold geometric architectural detail, an empty ornate ceremonial chair as central focal point, dusk light through tall lattice windows, formal mourning reception atmosphere, muted crowd of robed nobles and clergy, photorealistic, cinematic wide shot.

## Room 4 — Private Antechamber (Act Two)

**Key elements:** Small, intimate — a private meeting room for two people and tea service. Warmer and more personal than the ceremonial rooms, closer in feeling to the Sultan's Chamber. This is where Bashir and Fatima have their pivotal private scene.

> **AI image prompt:** Small intimate palace antechamber, two low seats and a tea service, warm indigo and gold accents against earth-toned walls, soft afternoon light through a single lattice window, private and quiet, photorealistic interior, Sahel-inspired palace architecture.

## Room 5 — Corridor (Act Two)

**Key elements:** A functional connecting space — should look distinct from the ceremonial rooms (plainer, narrower) so Yusuf's letter-passing scene reads as happening in the palace's "in-between" spaces rather than its performed spaces.

> **AI image prompt:** Plain functional palace corridor, narrower and less ornamented than ceremonial rooms, earth-toned walls, occasional small lattice window, muted daylight, quiet and transitional, photorealistic interior, Sahel-inspired architecture.

## Room 6 — Reception Room (Act Three)

**Key elements:** Formal but smaller than the Great Hall — a negotiation space. Should read as a room built to project calm authority to a visiting dignitary (Amina), with untouched tea as a key prop.

> **AI image prompt:** Formal but modestly scaled palace reception room, two seats facing each other with an untouched tea service between them, indigo and gold geometric wall detail, controlled midday light through lattice windows, composed and slightly tense atmosphere, photorealistic interior.

## Room 7 — Elder Bashir's Study (Act Three, Act Four)

**Key elements:** Distinct from the rest of the Palace — this is Bashir's personal space, should feel scholarly and cluttered with ledgers rather than palace-grand, closer in spirit to a University office than a royal room. Candlelit at night (Act Three); cold dawn light in Act Four with the death tableau — see GMU-407 (Elder Bashir character sheet) for staging notes on the body itself.

> **AI image prompt:** Small cluttered scholar's study within a palace, shelves and stacks of ledgers, a modest writing desk, warm candlelight at night, earth-toned walls with minimal ornamentation compared to ceremonial palace rooms. Dawn variant: cold blue-grey morning light through a single window, an overturned inkwell on the desk, quiet and still. Photorealistic interior, Sahel-inspired architecture.

## Exterior — Main Gate (Act Three)

**Key elements:** The Palace's formal arrival point — grand enough for a river-barge procession to arrive with visible ceremony. Emerald and white Kwara banners should read as a deliberate color intrusion against the palace's indigo/gold.

> **AI image prompt:** Grand formal palace gate and entrance steps, earth-toned stone with indigo and gold geometric detail, wide enough for a ceremonial arrival procession, a river-barge landing visible nearby with emerald and white banners contrasting the palace's indigo and gold color scheme, daylight, photorealistic wide exterior shot.
