# GMU-002 — Creative & Brand Bible

Estimated full length: ~250 pages. This v0.2 draft formalizes the visual identity that was already made implicitly across the World Bible (GMU-004), the Episode 1 character design sheets, and the location concept briefs — rather than inventing a visual system from nothing, this document consolidates what Episode 1's production package already committed to, fills the gaps (official color codes, typography, cinematography rules), and becomes the single reference everything downstream must match.

## 1. Official GMU Studio Identity

GMU Studio's own identity (distinct from the show's) stays minimal and functional at this stage: wordmark "GMU STUDIO" in the same display typeface as the show's logo (Section 3), rendered in neutral charcoal/white rather than any kingdom's palette, since the studio sits above all three fictional factions. A full studio brand package (letterhead, pitch deck template, social identity) is deferred until the studio has external-facing needs — not required for Episode 1 production.

## 2. The GIDAN MULKI Logo System

**Primary wordmark:** "GIDAN MULKI" set in a custom display treatment blending geometric Sahelian ornamentation (angular, symmetric, drawn from the architectural language in Section 6) with calligraphic flow reminiscent of the region's manuscript traditions — not a literal script font, an original geometric-calligraphic hybrid so the brand doesn't lean on any single real-world writing system.

**Icon mark:** A compass rose — chosen because the franchise is fundamentally about power moving between three centers (Section 4), and a compass is the clearest possible visual shorthand for "where does legitimacy point." This is also Zamar's own sigil element (open book beneath a compass rose, per GMU-004 §2.1), which is intentional: Zamar's symbol becomes the neutral franchise mark specifically because Zamar's founding premise — legitimacy through law rather than force — is the show's thesis. The compass rose alone (without the book) is the extracted franchise-level icon; the book stays exclusive to Zamar's own kingdom seal (Section 5).

**Lockup rules:** Wordmark and icon may appear together (icon left, wordmark right) for title cards and posters, or wordmark alone for in-body references. Icon never appears alone as a standalone logo — it's a Zamar/franchise dual-use symbol only in combination with the wordmark, to avoid confusing "the franchise" with "just Zamar" (a distinction the show's own politics depend on).

**Color treatment:** Indigo and gold by default (matching Zamar/franchise crossover), with a monochrome white/charcoal variant for use over photography or video.

## 3. Typography and Brand Standards

**Display typeface direction:** The custom wordmark treatment described in Section 2, used only for the logo itself and major title cards — never set as running text.

**Heading typeface:** A geometric sans-serif with slightly extended proportions, used for episode titles, character name cards, and section headers across all production documents and any audience-facing materials (pitch deck, website). Should read as clean and modern rather than trying to look "ancient" — the show's visual identity communicates its setting through architecture and color, not through faux-historical lettering.

**Body typeface:** A highly legible serif or humanist sans for subtitles, on-screen text, and long-form reading (scripts, this bible, the website). Prioritize legibility over character — subtitles in particular need to disappear into readability.

**Rule:** No typeface in this system should visually mimic a specific real-world script (Arabic calligraphy, N'Ko, Ajami, etc.) as a costume — GMU-001's founding principle of "inspired by, not replicating" applies to typography exactly as it applies to the fictional kingdoms.

**Rule:** A location or character's dominant palette should be identifiable in a single still frame without needing dialogue or a title card to confirm which kingdom it belongs to. If a scene's color grading doesn't pass that test, it's not matching this system yet.

## 5. Flags, Banners, and Royal Seals

**Zamar:** An open book beneath a compass rose, indigo field, gold linework. Formal seal (used on documents, ratification proclamations) adds a laurel-like geometric border; the banner version (physical flags, per GMU-701's screenplay descriptions) drops the border for a cleaner silhouette readable at distance.

**Kasar Dutse:** Crossed spears over a stone peak, deep red field, iron-grey linework. No formal "document seal" variant needed yet — Kasar Dutse's succession law is unwritten (GMU-004 §2.2), so it has no equivalent bureaucratic seal culture. Worth revisiting once the Political Bible is drafted.

**Kwara:** A silver river-fish, emerald field, minimal linework — deliberately the simplest of the three marks, reinforcing Kwara's "we're just merchants, nothing to perform" self-image (GMU-004 §2.3, GMU-404).

## 6. Architectural Language

Consolidated from the location concept briefs (GMU-501 through GMU-505) — this section is now the authoritative version; the location briefs should be read as its first applied examples.

**Shared principle across all three kingdoms:** earth-toned base materials (clay, sandstone, grey stone depending on kingdom) with geometric — never figurative — ornamentation. No architecture in this world depicts human or animal figures decoratively; pattern work is abstract and geometric throughout, a consistent world-building rule worth locking now before more locations get designed.

**Zamar:** Indigo and gold tile inlay over earth-toned or white-plastered walls, lattice windows casting patterned light, domes and terraced construction. Reads as rich but restrained — the ornamentation is precise rather than lavish, reinforcing Zamar's "law substitutes for force" self-concept (GMU-004 §2.1).

**Kasar Dutse:** Grey stone, minimal ornamentation, function over display. Where Zamar's architecture performs authority through precision, Kasar Dutse's performs it through scale and material honesty — thick walls, exposed stone, nothing decorative that doesn't also serve defense.

**Kwara (expanded 2026-07-11, now locked against GMU-507's Kwarafi Merchant House brief):** Light timber and pale stone construction, open canal-facing walls rather than enclosed courtyards — Kwara builds for the movement of goods, people, and information, not for defense (Kasar Dutse) or ceremony (Zamar). Airy, low-mass structures over monumental ones; where Zamar's architecture performs law through precision and Kasar Dutse performs strength through mass, Kwara performs **transparency and liquidity** — visible ledgers, constant clerk activity, moving water always in frame. Roofs and walkways should read as built for a river-delta trade economy: raised timber walkways over canals, awnings and shade structures for open-air market activity, minimal load-bearing stone reserved for foundations and ground floors. Ornamentation, where it exists, is functional rather than decorative — ledger-shelving, scale-and-measure fixtures, cargo winches — Kwara doesn't spend material wealth performing status the way Zamar or Kasar Dutse do; per GMU-404/GMU-507, its wealth signals through operational competence, not display.

## 7. Interior Design of Palaces

Zamar's Palace interior language is fully specified in GMU-502 — this section elevates that brief's shared language to canon status: low, dense-wood, minimally carved furniture; indigo/gold tile inlay as the primary decorative device; deep-set lattice windows. Kasar Dutse's interiors remain undesigned (only exteriors and yards have appeared on screen) — defer until an episode requires them.

**Kwara (expanded 2026-07-11, per GMU-507):** Kwara's seat of power, the Kwarafi Merchant House, sets the interior standard: light timber furnishings rather than Zamar's dense carved wood, open canal-facing windows rather than Zamar's lattice screens, ledger-shelves and clerks' desks in place of Zamar's scripture and Kasar Dutse's weapon racks. A recurring tea-service prop (deliberately echoing Yusuf's and Bashir's own tea habits elsewhere in the series — a visual rhyme worth preserving in any future Kwara scene) signals transactional hospitality rather than genuine intimacy. Amina's private office is the template for Kwara's "personal but still professional" register: still transactional in feel even at its most private, nothing in the room exists purely for comfort or display. Kwara's throne-equivalent, if the story ever needs one (a formal seat of Confederacy-wide authority beyond Amina's own counting hall), should follow the same "raised desk, not a throne" logic already established in Room 1 — authority performed through visible competence, not elevation or ornament.

## 8. Weapons and Armor Styles

Provisional, pending the Military Bible (`04_WORLD_BIBLE/MILITARY_BIBLE/`):

**Zamar:** Cavalry-standard gear, functional rather than ceremonial even at officer rank (see GMU-402, GMU-408) — Zamar's military doesn't need to visually compete with its own scholarly institutions for legitimacy.

**Kasar Dutse:** Iron and leather, practical mountain-warfare design, minimal decoration matching the kingdom's architectural honesty (Section 6).

**Kwara (expanded 2026-07-11):** The Confederacy fields "a mercenary fleet" rather than a standing army (GMU-004 §2.3), so Kwara's weapon and armor conventions should read as naval/marine rather than cavalry: boarding weapons (short blades, boat-hooks repurposed as polearms) over the long spears and swords seen in Zamar and Kasar Dutse's forces; light, flexible leather-and-cloth protection suited to riverine combat and boat mobility rather than Kasar Dutse's heavier plate/mail; emerald-and-silver trim kept minimal and functional (a sash or armband rather than full heraldic dress) consistent with Kwara's broader "competence over display" principle (Section 6). Since Kwara's soldiers are hired rather than raised from its own population, expect visible variation in individual gear within any single unit — a mercenary company should never read as visually uniform the way Zamar's or Kasar Dutse's standing forces do; that inconsistency is itself the correct design signal, not an error to fix.

## 9. Fashion Guides by Region and Social Class

Consolidated from the nine Episode 1 character sheets (GMU-401 through GMU-409):

**Zamar — scholarly/court class:** Floor-length robes, structured shoulders, geometric gold trim restricted to cuffs/hem (never full-garment embroidery — see Fatima's and Bashir's sheets for the restraint principle). Rank is communicated through sash color and trim density, not garment shape.

**Zamar — military class:** Dark tunics, gold braid marking rank at shoulder/cuff, practical cut. See Malik, Danladi, and Tahir's sheets for the rank-escalation logic (more braid, more seniority).

**Kasar Dutse:** Deep red and iron-grey layered garments, minimal ornament even for the King (see Garba's sheet) — status communicated through bearing and quality of material rather than visible decoration, consistent with the kingdom's "material honesty" architectural principle.

**Kwara:** Tailored rather than draped, fine plain fabric with subtle sheen rather than embroidery (see Amina's sheet) — wealth signaled through cut and material quality, never through visible symbols of rank.

**Cross-kingdom rule established in Episode 1:** the unbleached mourning-white overlay (worn during the succession crisis by every Zamar character regardless of rank) is a leveling garment — a useful device worth reusing whenever the story needs to visually flatten status differences for a scene.

**Not yet designed:** everyday/civilian fashion by profession (deferred to `04_WORLD_BIBLE/CLOTHING/`), and any Kasar Dutse or Kwara costume below the "named principal character" level.

## 10. Cinematography Style

Not yet tested against real footage, but establishing a baseline direction so early generated shots stay consistent:

**Zamar / institutional scenes (Diwan Hall, University, formal court):** Stable, often symmetric framing; slower push-ins rather than handheld movement. The camera should feel like it trusts the room — reinforcing that these are spaces where process and law, not chaos, are supposed to govern.

**Military / urgency scenes (cavalry grounds, Tsauri Keep mobilization, the Act One rider interruption):** Slightly more mobile camera, tracking movement rather than holding static — still controlled, not shaky-cam, but with more kinetic energy than the institutional scenes.

**Intimate two-handers (the antechamber scene with Bashir, the Sultan's Chamber scene with the siblings):** Closer lenses, shallower depth of field, minimal camera movement — let performance carry these scenes rather than camera work.

**General rule:** camera restraint scales with how much the scene is about law/institution versus how much it's about raw power or intimacy. This mirrors the season's central thesis (does law hold under pressure) at the level of shot language, not just story.

## 11. Lighting Philosophy

**Zamar:** Warm, golden-amber interior light (lattice-filtered, per GMU-502), cooler and mistier for exteriors at dawn (per GMU-501's River Port brief). Warmth signals institutional stability; Episode 1 deliberately keeps this warmth even during the Act Four murder scene's dawn light (cold blue-grey) specifically to signal that the warmth has been violated.

**Kasar Dutse:** Overcast, harsher, higher-contrast mountain light throughout (per GMU-505) — no warm interior equivalent has been designed yet since no Kasar Dutse interior has appeared on screen.

**Kwara (expanded 2026-07-11, per GMU-507):** Soft, reflected water-light — Kwara's canal-facing architecture means most interior light arrives indirectly, bounced off moving water, giving scenes a gentle, shifting quality distinct from Zamar's fixed lattice-patterned glow or Kasar Dutse's harsh overcast mountain light. Daytime scenes lean warm-neutral (per GMU-507's Counting Hall and Trade Quarter briefs); the one established night scene (Amina's balcony) uses warm lamplight reflected on water below against cool moonlit timber — intimate rather than cold, a deliberate contrast to the "cleaner, cooler" instinct that might otherwise seem to fit Amina's controlled self-presentation. Kwara's light should feel alive and in motion even in still frames, reinforcing Section 6's "transparency and liquidity" principle at the level of lighting, not just architecture.

## 12. Music and Sound Identity

Full detail belongs in `04_WORLD_BIBLE/MUSIC/` (not yet drafted) and eventually a dedicated soundtrack bible — this section only sets provisional direction so temp scoring doesn't contradict later canon:

**Zamar:** Should lean toward string and light percussion instrumentation, unhurried tempo, reflecting scholarly restraint.

**Kasar Dutse:** Lower brass and heavier percussion, sparser arrangement, reflecting martial starkness.

**Kwara:** Lighter, more melodic instrumentation (strings, woodwinds), reflecting cosmopolitan ease.

**Rule:** avoid direct pastiche of any single real-world regional music tradition, consistent with GMU-001's "inspired by, not replicating" principle — same logic already applied to language (GMU-004 §Language) and typography (Section 3).

## 13. AI Visual Prompt Standards

Extracted from the pattern already used consistently across all nine Episode 1 character sheets and five location briefs — this formalizes that pattern as the house style for every future prompt:

**Standard prompt structure:** [subject/setting] → [distinguishing physical/architectural features] → [costume/material details] → [palette, referencing Section 4's locked hex values by name] → [lighting, referencing Section 11] → [mood/atmosphere] → [technical tags: "photorealistic," "cinematic," "consistent character reference sheet" or "wide establishing shot," etc.]

**Consistency rule:** every prompt for a recurring character or location should reference back to that character's or location's locked design sheet rather than being freshly re-described from memory — this is why GMU-401 through GMU-505 exist as standalone, reusable documents rather than being embedded only in the shot list.

**Negative-prompt guidance (for tools that support it):** avoid generic "fantasy" or "medieval European" visual defaults that AI image models often default to — explicitly steer toward the Sahel-inspired, geometric-not-figurative aesthetic established in Section 6, since this is the detail most likely to drift without active correction.

## 14. Official Poster and Title Sequence Style

**Provisional direction (not yet produced):** Title card built around the compass rose icon (Section 2), with the wordmark resolving in from a rotation or reveal animation, background cycling briefly through fragments of the three kingdoms' palettes before settling on the neutral franchise gold/indigo — visually enacting the show's premise (three powers, one contested center) in the first few seconds of every episode. Full poster and main-title sequence production deferred until Episode 1 has produced footage to cut a title sequence against.
