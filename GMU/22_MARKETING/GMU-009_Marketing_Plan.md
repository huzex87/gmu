# GMU-009 — Marketing Plan

Positioning, audience, and go-to-market plan for Gidan Mulki Universe, built against GMU-001's Scope (§3), Strategic Objectives (§4), Success Metrics (§5), and Audience (§8) — and against what's actually true about the project today: a complete, production-ready Season One design package (20 chapters, full worldbuilding suite, full prompt library) with no footage produced yet. This plan is written for that real starting point, not an aspirational one.

## 1. Positioning

**Core positioning statement:** Gidan Mulki is an original African political epic — not a retelling of any real historical empire, not a fantasy-genre pastiche, but a Sahel-inspired world built with the same seriousness of craft as a prestige political drama, using AI production tools to make a franchise of this scope achievable by a small team rather than a legacy studio.

**Differentiation (per GMU-001 §9, Franchise Position):** The project deliberately avoids emulating existing franchises. Its distinctiveness rests on three things this document should keep foregrounding in every marketing asset: (1) a genuinely original setting grounded in real Sahelian history and Islamic governance rather than invented wholesale or borrowed from elsewhere, (2) a political-drama structure where "politics before violence" and "no character is entirely good or evil" (GMU-001 §6) are load-bearing creative rules, not marketing copy, and (3) an AI-native production model that is itself part of the story worth telling to investors and press — a small team producing franchise-scale output.

**What NOT to lead with:** Avoid positioning this primarily as "an AI-generated show" to a general audience — GMU-001 §10's production philosophy is explicit that AI accelerates creativity without replacing human judgment on story, ethics, culture, and performance. Audience-facing marketing should lead with the story and world; the AI production story is a genuinely compelling angle for industry, investor, and press audiences specifically (Section 4 below), not the general public.

## 2. Target Market Breakdown (per GMU-001 §8)

**Primary audience:** Ages 18–45, drawn to political drama, epic-scale worldbuilding, and prestige television — the same audience that responds to dense, multi-POV political narratives rather than pure spectacle.

**Secondary audience:** Ages 15+, drawn in through the franchise's broader multimedia expansion (Section 5 below, and GMU-010) — gaming, publishing, and educational content have a lower age floor than the television series itself.

**Global target markets (per GMU-001 §8):**

| Market | Positioning angle |
|---|---|
| West Africa | Cultural pride and representation angle — an original epic built from real regional history and governance traditions, not a foreign lens on the region |
| North Africa | Shared Islamic governance and Sahelian trade history resonance (per GMU-305's research-grounded religious basis) |
| Middle East | Shared faith-tradition resonance; positioning should lean on GMU-305's research-grounded, respectful treatment of Islamic governance and jurisprudence as a genuine point of trust-building, not just accuracy |
| Europe | Prestige political drama angle — comparable to how European audiences engage with dense, subtitled political epics |
| North America | Original-IP and "next big fantasy/political epic" angle, positioned against the gap left by ended prestige political dramas |
| Asia | Epic-scale worldbuilding and franchise-multimedia angle (gaming/publishing expansion, per GMU-010) rather than television-first |

## 3. Pitch Deck Content Plan (feeds `23_PITCH/`)

The pitch deck should be built once GMU-002's visual identity is fully locked (currently in progress, Kwara's visual language closed 2026-07-11) and should draw directly from existing, real repository content rather than invented marketing copy:

1. **Logline and premise** — pulled from START_HERE.md §2's 60-second story, tightened to a single logline plus a one-paragraph premise.
2. **World and tone** — 3–5 key concept art images once Phase 1 image generation (GMU-006 §2) produces locked location references, paired with GMU-002's palette/architecture system.
3. **Character lineup** — the 6 principal cast members' AI Model Library reference images (once generated) paired with one-line character hooks pulled from GMU-005.
4. **Season One structure** — the 20-chapter format itself is a pitch point: shorter, cliffhanger-driven chapters suited to modern streaming consumption patterns, not just an AI-production convenience.
5. **Sample material** — Chapter 1's screenplay excerpt and, once available, a produced sample scene or teaser (GMU-006 §4's recommended Chapter 01–02 priority generation order).
6. **The production model** — a short, compelling explanation of the AI-assisted pipeline (GMU-006) as a genuine differentiator for cost-conscious buyers/investors, per Section 1's note that this angle belongs in industry-facing materials.
7. **Franchise scope** — a compressed version of GMU-010's multimedia roadmap, establishing this isn't pitched as a single season but a franchise.
8. **The ask** — funding/partnership specifics, to be filled in once GMU-010's business plan (Section 3 there) is finalized.

## 4. Investor Materials Plan (feeds `24_INVESTORS/`)

Per GMU-010, the investor package should include: a compressed franchise plan summary, comparable-titles analysis (prestige political dramas and franchise-scale original IP, to be researched at the time materials are actually built rather than speculated here), the production cost model (drawing directly from GMU-006 §10's rough per-season cost estimate), and a clear funding ask tied to GMU-001 §4's Year 1/2/3 roadmap — Year Two's "Investor Package" milestone is explicitly when this becomes a real deliverable, not before.

## 5. Social/Community Strategy

**Pre-launch (Year One, per GMU-001 §4):** Build audience around the worldbuilding itself — the depth of GMU-004's fourteen sub-bibles, GMU-005's character roster, and GMU-311's Atlas geography are genuine content assets that can be shared incrementally (a location reveal, a character reveal, a world-lore thread) well before any footage exists, building anticipation the way a novel series or comic might, rather than waiting for a trailer to start engaging an audience.

**Digital hub (per GMU-001 §3's Digital scope):** The planned interactive website, character database, interactive timeline, and interactive map are the long-term home for this content — until built, social channels function as the interim distribution surface for the same worldbuilding material.

**Community engagement tone:** Consistent with GMU-001 §6's creative principles ("culture should be lived, not explained," "history shapes every present-day decision") — community content should invite audience members into the world's internal logic (a succession-law explainer, a "which kingdom are you" framing) rather than simply announcing production milestones.
